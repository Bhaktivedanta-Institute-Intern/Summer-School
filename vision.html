Objective

The Foundations of Science
by Henri Poincare
Picture by Russell McNeil licensed under CC BY 2.0

The Quest to understand nature through scientific methods has taken deep roots since the past few centuries. With the development of new tools and technologies, exploration has ventured across a wide scale, from subatomic particles to galaxies. However, when we focus on the foundations of science and mathematics, we encounter various unknowns in every discipline - ranging from mysteries of space and time in cosmology to intricacies in quantum mechanics; in foundations of mathematics and computer science ranging from enigma of imaginary numbers to the limits of logic; in foundations of biology from the unfathomable riddles inside the cell to the most evident but mysterious ‘consciousness’; in foundations of psychology from unknowns in perception and cognition to personal traits and much more.

What is even more surprising besides various unknowns in science, is the ‘unknowables’ - which cannot be known at all. Sounds bizarre? But it's true. These are not merely some external blockages in our workings we encounter in our laboratories, but rather intrinsic limits found in the very discipline itself. Gӧdel’s theorem in mathematics, the Uncertainty principle in physics and Turing’s halting problem in computer science are famous examples of these impossibility results. Similarly, in spirituality, many elements, including God, can never be comprehended ‘fully’ by our limited brains.

The present summer school will delve into this domain of unknowns and unknowables across various fields of foundations of science and mathematics. Its objectives are i) to inspire and expose the audience to the amazing foundational concepts and their limits prevailing in various subjects of science in exploring the nature and its underlying principles ii) to educate them on what further can be known and what will remain unknown forever iii) to explore the foundational principles (if any) which the present-day science might be missing in its search for the Ultimate Reality, taking guidance from ancient - spiritual traditions. The school thus aims to thoroughly introduce serious students and scholars to the science of known, unknowns and unknowables.


Dr. T. D. Singh in conversation with Eminent Scientists


For centuries the reductive model has been supreme . . . But over the waning years of the twentieth century a return of interest in holistic issues has begun to emerge.

Dr.Linus Pauling, Nobel Laureate in Chemistry (1954) and Peace (1962)

. . . one great question that has dominated my life is: “What am I?” What is the meaning of this marvellous gift of life? The more we know, the more the mystery grows.

Sir John Eccles, Nobel Laureate in Physiology and Medicine (1963)

In my view, the question of origin seems to be left unanswered if we explore from a scientific view alone. Thus, I believe there is a need for some religious or metaphysical explanation . . .

Prof. Charles Townes, Nobel Laureate in Physics (1964)

I come toward the end of my life as a scientist facing two great problems (cosmology of life and consciousness) . . . I believe that both are irrevocably forever unassimilable as science.

Prof. George Wald, Nobel Laureate in Physiology and Medicine (1967)

. . . I think the mystery of life is till to conceive how these organic molecules, which are already similar or equal to what we know today to represent components of organic life, come together such that some living primordial cell may become functioning . . .

Prof. Werner Arber, Nobel Laureate in Physiology and Medicine (1978)

We cannot understand biology, if we do not understand how it could have begun. We cannot truly know chemistry if we cannot how it could give rise to biology.

Prof. Paul Lauterbur, Nobel Laureate in Physiology and Medicine (2003)

. . . we certainly don’t believe that consciousness can be understood in terms of present day science.

Sir Roger Penrose, Nobel Laureate in Physics (2020)

. . . Irreducuible complexity is a challenge to the Darwinian explanation of life … It shows why Darwianian explanation won’t work with many of the structures we find in the cell.

Prof. Micheal Behe, Professor of Biochemistry at Lehigh University, Pennsylvania

For centuries the reductive model has been supreme . . . But over the waning years of the twentieth century a return of interest in holistic issues has begun to emerge.

Karl H. Pribram, professor at Georgetown University, an emeritus professor at Stanford University and distinguished professor at Radford University.
